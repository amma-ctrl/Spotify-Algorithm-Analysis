Methodology: Data, Metrics, and Ethical Framing

Dataset

This study uses the 900K+ Spotify Songs Dataset from Kaggle. It includes genre tags, emotional labels, playlist types, and audio features (danceability, valence, etc.). Though not tied to user history, it reveals Spotify's structural logic.

My Approach

  • Similarity Pairing: Compare recommended songs to originals using cluster IDs.
  • Entropy & Looping Scores: Calculate the diversity of genre and emotion and measure how often acoustic features were repeated.
  • PCA & Transitions: Visualize audio clustering and tracked genre/emotion shifts.

Ethics

All data is public, anonymized, and used solely for analytical purposes. No personal user data was accessed.

Results & Discussion

Spotify Preserves Mood, Not Style

The first pattern to emerge from the analysis was the disparity between emotional and genre similarity in Spotify‚Äôs recommendations. Only 24% of the recommended songs matched the genre of the original song, while 42% matched the emotional classification. This suggests that Spotify‚Äôs algorithm prioritizes emotional continuity over stylistic exploration. In other words, the platform seeks to preserve how a song makes you feel more than how it sounds on a musical genre level.

This emotional consistency creates a listening environment where users remain within a familiar affective zone, even if the musical style shifts. For example, someone listening to a melancholic indie track may next hear a soft acoustic ballad from a different genre, but the emotional register remains consistent. This helps maintain user engagement by offering subtle novelty without destabilizing the overall listening experience.

Diversity in Sound, Uniformity in Feeling

Genre entropy was high (6.33), but emotional entropy was low (2.02). While users may hear a range of genres, the feelings evoked remain narrowly consistent. Spotify offers micro-discovery: different styles, the same vibes.

Behind the Music: PCA Shows Acoustic Looping

PCA mapping revealed that recommended songs cluster closely in acoustic space. Genres like EDM and Hip Hop form dense sonic zones, reinforcing that musical novelty often masks acoustic repetition.

Emotion transitions mostly followed ‚Äúdiagonal‚Äù paths (e.g., joy ‚Üí joy), confirming a loop logic. Genre transitions took leaps, like country to hip hop, but were tied to crossover artists or similar tempo profiles‚Äîshowing that even deviations are controlled.

Interpretation

Spotify‚Äôs algorithm encourages what I call ‚Äúmicro-exploration‚Äù. The platform introduces new songs that differ on paper‚Äîbut sound and feel remarkably similar. This maintains user comfort while limiting exposure to unfamiliar emotional or sonic landscapes.

Limitations & Future Work

  • Dataset does not reflect actual listening behavior over time.
  • No segmentation between editorial, user, and algorithmic playlists.
  • No demographic breakdowns (age, location, language, etc.).

Future research could explore genre stickiness, song release effects, or cross-cultural differences using behavioral APIs or user surveys.
