Loop or Discover?
How Spotify's Algorithms Shape the Soundtrack of Our Lives

By Amen Hailu

Introduction: A Platform That Knows Your Soundtrack

In the age of streaming, algorithms aren’t just personalizing our choices, they’re curating our emotional experiences. With over 600 million global users, Spotify has emerged as one of the most influential platforms in digital media. Its recommendation engine determines not only what we listen to next, but how we feel, move, and even remember. As our daily soundtracks become algorithmically guided, the boundary between preference and programming grows increasingly blurred.

This research investigates Spotify’s algorithmic recommendations with a central question in mind:

Does exposure to Spotify’s algorithmic recommendations lead to a broader range of musical genres over time, or does it result in a more limited and repetitive listening pattern?

In an environment where digital platforms wield increasing cultural power, this project explores whether Spotify promotes genuine musical exploration, or subtly encourages repetition under the guise of novelty. The implications extend far beyond listening habits: they touch on artist visibility, emotional regulation, and the future of cultural diversity in automated spaces.

Background Research: Echo Chambers & Filter Bubbles

Recommendation systems across platforms like YouTube, TikTok, and Netflix have come under scrutiny for reinforcing “filter bubbles”, personalized loops that limit exposure to unfamiliar content. In the realm of music, researchers like Celma (2010) and Nguyen et al. (2014) have described the emergence of “musical filter bubbles,” warning that algorithms might prioritize convenience over cultural or artistic expansion.

Spotify’s hybrid model, which blends collaborative filtering, natural language processing, and audio feature clustering, makes it a uniquely powerful case study. Yet while it has been praised for its personalization, few empirical studies have explored whether Spotify actually encourages discovery or traps users in emotional or stylistic loops. This project seeks to extend that gap in the literature through a large-scale analysis of Spotify recommendation structures using publicly available data.

Methodology: Data, Metrics, and Ethical Framing

To analyze Spotify’s algorithmic logic, I used the publicly available 900K+ Spotify Songs Dataset from Kaggle (published by DevDope). This dataset contains metadata for over 900,000 tracks, including genre labels, emotional classifications, playlist tags, similarity clusters, and audio features such as danceability, valence, and energy. While the dataset does not include user behavior, it provides a strong proxy for examining Spotify’s internal recommendation patterns.

For my approach I used three primary methods to guided the analysis:

  • Similarity Pairing: Tracks were grouped using cluster IDs to compare original songs with their recommended counterparts. This made it possible to evaluate how often Spotify maintained or altered musical features like genre and emotion.
  • Exploration vs. Repetition Metrics:
    • I used Shannon Entropy to assess diversity in genre and emotion across tracks.
    • I calculated a looping score based on how frequently recommended songs remained within the same audio feature cluster as previous tracks.
  • Dimensional and Visual Analysis:
    • PCA (Principal Component Analysis) visualizations mapped songs in two-dimensional acoustic space.
    • Genre and Emotion Transition Heatmaps highlighted how often listeners were nudged across emotional or stylistic boundaries.

Lastly, but most importantly, all data is anonymized and publicly accessible. The research adheres to principles of ethical transparency, reproducibility, and the use of open-source data.

Results & Discussion

Spotify Preserves Mood, Not Style

My analysis began by comparing how often Spotify recommendations matched the genre or emotion of the original song. The results were revealing: only 24% of recommendations matched the original track’s genre, while 42% matched its emotional tone. This suggests that Spotify prioritizes emotional alignment over stylistic diversity. In practice, this means a user listening to a nostalgic indie song may be guided to an acoustic folk ballad rather than an upbeat hip-hop track, because the emotional tone is preserved.

This mood-first logic reflects Spotify’s investment in listener retention. Emotional continuity keeps users engaged without overwhelming them with jarring transitions. Yet it also subtly limits the types of emotional experiences listeners are likely to encounter.

Diversity in Sound, Uniformity in Feeling

To assess how diverse Spotify’s recommendations truly are, I calculated their entropy scores. The results show a disparity: genre entropy was relatively high (6.33), suggesting a broad stylistic range, but emotional entropy was significantly lower (2.02). Even across themed playlists such as “Good for Study” or “Party,” the emotional variety remained narrow.

These findings point to a model of micro-discovery: Spotify may expose users to new genres or unfamiliar artists, but it keeps the emotional register tightly controlled. As a result, novelty exists within well-defined affective boundaries, reinforcing familiar feelings even as musical styles vary.

Behind the Music: PCA Shows Acoustic Looping

The PCA audio feature visualization provided another layer of insight. By mapping audio characteristics (like tempo, loudness, and energy), the PCA plot showed that recommended songs often cluster closely with the original. Genres such as EDM and Hip Hop formed especially tight clusters, indicating that even when genre labels differed, songs often shared very similar acoustic properties.

This finding suggests that Spotify’s algorithm engineers an illusion of change. A track might be labeled as "Alternative" rather than "Pop," but it will likely feature the same mid-tempo beat, similar vocal tone, or consistent rhythm. The underlying acoustic sameness creates a smooth listening experience, one that subtly discourages true exploration.

Transition Diagrams: Safe Steps Across Familiar Borders

Emotion and genre transition heatmaps illustrated how recommendations flow from one category to another. Most transitions followed diagonal paths (joy to joy) indicating that Spotify typically recommends songs within the same category. However, some interesting genre trends emerged: for instance, country to hip hop, or indie to R&B.

These exceptions could be associated with crossover artists or shared tempos. Rather than serving as examples of radical discovery, they seem more like algorithmically acceptable leaps, safe steps across familiar borders, guided by audio similarity or mainstream popularity.

Interpretation: Loops Disguised as Exploration

Collectively, these results point to a Spotify ecosystem optimized for micro-discovery and emotional stability. The platform favors small acoustic and emotional deviations rather than bold departures. This design creates a listening experience that feels personalized, smooth, and comforting, but may also limit the potential for cultural, emotional, and artistic expansion.

Spotify is not locking users into strict echo chambers, but it is guiding them through algorithmically designed corridors of taste. As users, we feel like we’re exploring, when in fact, we’re looping through a series of tightly connected musical islands.

Limitations & Future Work

While the findings offer valuable insights, several limitations remain:

  • This study uses metadata and cluster similarity rather than actual user behavior. Without logs from features like Discover Weekly, I can’t observe real-time exploration patterns.
  • The dataset doesn’t differentiate between user-generated, editorial, and algorithmic playlists, each of which may operate under different recommendation logic.
  • Laslty, without regional or demographic data, the research doesn’t account for cultural or geographic variation in musical exposure.

Future directions include analyzing the "stickiness" of certain genres, tracking the role of song age in discovery, and comparing exploration tendencies across countries or listener demographics if Spotify API or behavioral data becomes available.

Conclusion: Algorithmic Curation and the Boundaries of Taste

Spotify is a master of musical mood management. Its algorithm doesn’t fence users into rigid filter bubbles, but it gently shepherds them through familiar emotional terrain. While users may believe they are discovering new music, they are often being introduced to sonically similar, emotionally familiar content that reinforces established preferences.

This study shows that Spotify promotes a curated form of curiosity: one that balances novelty with emotional comfort. For listeners, this can be soothing. For independent artists and genre diversity, it raises concerns about visibility and cultural homogenization.

As music streaming platforms continue to shape global listening habits, transparency and scrutiny of algorithmic design will be key to ensuring that digital discovery truly lives up to its name.

Spotify promotes exploration, but only within the bounds of familiarity.
